# Cultural Weak Areas

Standing list of **cultural** weak spots to quiz proactively — customs,
etiquette, social context (things about *how Japan works*, not how the language
works). See `CLAUDE.md` ("Known weak areas") for the instruction to work these
into practice without being asked. Add to this file as new cultural gaps come up
(class discussion, self-identified, or flagged by the girlfriend/teacher).

**Language** weak spots (kana, vocab, grammar, readings, particles) live in the
sibling file `language_weak_areas.md`. Items with both angles are logged in both,
cross-referenced.

## バレンタインデー / ホワイトデー (Valentine's Day / White Day)

Flagged from class (2026-07-18), via the sentence わたしは バレンタインデーに
かれに チョコレートを あげます (language angle: `language_weak_areas.md` →
"Grammar: あげます"). In Japan the gift direction is **gendered and split
across two days**:

- **バレンタインデー (Feb 14):** *women* give *men* chocolate — the reverse of
  the Western default. Two kinds worth knowing:
  - **本命チョコ (ほんめいチョコ)** — "true-feeling" chocolate for a romantic
    interest / partner.
  - **義理チョコ (ぎりチョコ)** — "obligation" chocolate given to male
    colleagues, bosses, friends out of social duty (義理 = obligation/duty),
    no romantic meaning.
- **ホワイトデー (Mar 14):** one month later, *men* reciprocate to women who
  gave them chocolate, traditionally with white sweets/gifts (cookies, white
  chocolate, marshmallows). "返し" (return gift) is the expectation.

Why it's a weak area: the direction (women→men first) and the 本命 vs 義理
distinction are non-obvious to Western learners and shape what a sentence like
the あげます example actually *implies* socially.

## お年玉 / 正月 vs 旧正月 (New Year money & which New Year)

Flagged from class (2026-07-18), via 旧正月に こどもに お年玉を あげます
(language angle: `language_weak_areas.md` → "Vocab: 旧正月 / お年玉").

- **お年玉（おとしだま）custom:** at New Year, adults/relatives give **children**
  small envelopes of money. It's the Japanese cousin of the Chinese 紅包
  (hóngbāo) / lucky money. Direction: adult → child (fits あげます, outward).
- **正月 vs 旧正月 — which New Year:** plain **正月（しょうがつ）** in Japan =
  the **Gregorian** New Year (Jan 1), the major national holiday; お年玉 is
  traditionally given then. **旧正月（きゅうしょうがつ）** = the **lunar** New
  Year (春節 style), which most of mainland Japan does *not* celebrate (Okinawa
  and some regions do). So the class sentence frames the custom in its
  *lunar-calendar* form — natural in a Chinese-cultural context, less so as a
  mainstream Japanese practice.

## Hierarchy dictates verb choice (keigo)

Flagged from class (2026-07-18): "you can't say あげます to a senior." The
cultural rule underneath is that **social hierarchy (先輩後輩 senpai/kōhai,
in-group vs superiors, staff→customer) changes which verb you use**, not just
whether you add です/ます. Politeness in Japanese is *relational* — it encodes
who is above/below whom, so choosing the wrong-level verb is a social misstep,
not just a grammar error. Language forms logged in `language_weak_areas.md` →
"Keigo ladder" (under the giving/receiving entry). Key instances:

- **Giving to a superior:** use さしあげる, not あげる (あげる implies "doing them
  a favor," presumptuous aimed upward).
- **Receiving from a superior:** use いただく, not もらう (humble — you lower
  yourself; also why meals start with いただきます).
- **A superior giving to you:** use くださる, not くれる (honorific — you raise
  them; also the root of ください "please give me").

Underlying split: **謙譲語 (humble)** lowers *your own* actions; **尊敬語
(honorific)** raises *the other person's* actions. You never raise yourself or
lower them.

## Giving/receiving verbs encode 内/外 (uchi/soto) — a worldview, not just grammar

Flagged from class (2026-07-18): "もらいました is a cultural thing." The
あげる/くれる/もらう system is not neutral like English "give/receive" — the
language **forces you to encode social perspective every time**: who is in your
**in-group (内, uchi)** vs **out-group (外, soto)**, and from whose side you're
telling it. (Language forms: `language_weak_areas.md` → giving/receiving entry.)

- **English is viewpoint-neutral:** "gave" / "received" say nothing about sides.
- **Japanese is viewpoint-obligatory:** a gift *toward* you or your in-group must
  use くれる/もらう; a gift you cannot describe with あげる if it points at your
  own side. Family = 内, so it counts as "me."
- **Why it's cultural:** 内/外 (in-group/out-group) is a core organizing idea in
  Japanese society — you humble your own group and elevate outsiders/superiors.
  The same relational logic drives keigo (see entry above). Choosing the verb is
  a social act of positioning yourself, not just picking a synonym.

Practical upshot: when a sentence involves giving/receiving, first ask **"whose
side is each person on, and whose side am I telling this from?"** — that decides
the verb before grammar does.
